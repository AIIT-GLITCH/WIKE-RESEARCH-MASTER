🥇 The Big Three — Start Here
💊
Cost Plus Drugs (Mark Cuban)
Manufacturing cost + 15% markup + $3 dispensing fee. Metformin: $3.60/month. Imatinib (cancer drug): $47/month vs $10,000 brand. The single most important drug pricing resource that exists. Check here first for any generic.
→ costplusdrugs.com
💚
GoodRx
Free coupon that works at most pharmacies. Often beats insurance copays for generics. Always compare — the same drug can vary 4x between pharmacies using GoodRx. Costco and Kroger pharmacies tend to be lowest.
→ goodrx.com
🆓
NeedyMeds
Master database of every patient assistance program, disease fund, and drug discount card. Free to use. If you can't afford your medication, search here before paying. Most PAPs cover income up to 400% of federal poverty level.
→ needymeds.org
🏛️ Government Programs
⭐
Medicare Extra Help (Low Income Subsidy)
If you're on Medicare and your income is under ~$22,590 (single) / ~$30,660 (couple): you may qualify for $0 premiums, $0–$10 copays on all your drugs. Millions of people are eligible and not enrolled. Apply at SSA.gov or call Social Security.
→ Apply at SSA.gov  |  📞 1-800-772-1213
🏥
340B Pharmacies — Federally Qualified Health Centers
Community health centers, rural health clinics, and other safety-net providers can purchase drugs at dramatically reduced 340B prices and pass those savings to patients. Income-based sliding scale. Find one near you.
→ findahealthcenter.hrsa.gov
🏪
Walmart $4 Generic List
Walmart sells hundreds of common generic medications for $4/month or $10/3 months. Includes many blood pressure, diabetes, cholesterol, and antibiotic medications. No prescription discount card needed — this is the shelf price.
→ walmart.com/pharmacy/clinicalservices/druglist

Insulin Help — Manufacturers
Eli Lilly: Insulin Value Program ($35/month cap, anyone). Novo Nordisk Patient Assistance: free insulin for qualifying patients. Sanofi Insulins Valyou Savings: savings program. Walmart ReliOn OTC insulin: $25/vial, no prescription needed for NPH and Regular.

💡 Things to tell your doctor

"Is there a generic equivalent?" — Generics are chemically identical to brand drugs. Always ask. Sometimes doctors prescribe brand because that's what they know, not because the generic is worse.

"Can you write for a 90-day supply?" — 90-day fills are cheaper per dose than 30-day. Mail-order pharmacies often offer additional discounts on 90-day supplies.

"Is there a therapeutic equivalent that costs less?" — Different drugs in the same class often have the same effect. One may be $400/month, another $8. Your doctor can usually substitute.

"Can you give me samples while I apply to the patient assistance program?" — Doctors receive free drug samples constantly. They can bridge you while paperwork processes.

"Should I check GoodRx before filling this?" — Many doctors don't know GoodRx exists or that it often beats insurance copays. Tell them you're checking it.
