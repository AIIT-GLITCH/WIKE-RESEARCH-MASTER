# International Legal Precedents
## Cases and Frameworks from Outside the US

## 1. EUROPEAN COURT OF HUMAN RIGHTS (ECHR)

### I.M. and Others v. Italy (2022) — FORCED VISITATION VIOLATES HUMAN RIGHTS
- **Key holding:** Found violation of Article 8 where children were COMPELLED to have contact with their father — an addict with history of aggressive behavior — without sessions in a secured environment with a psychologist present
- **DIRECT APPLICATION:** This case already establishes that forced visitation with an abuser violates the European Convention on Human Rights. Paper 110's Requirement 1 (harsh must stop) is already international law.

### Neulinger and Shuruk v. Switzerland (2010) — PLACEMENT STABILITY
- Grand Chamber: "In all decisions concerning children, their best interests must be paramount"
- Emphasized placement stability — uprooting a child settled for 4 years would have "serious consequences"
- **APPLICATION:** Placement changes are recognized by ECHR as harmful at the highest judicial level

### K.O. and V.M. v. Norway (2019) — CONTACT RESTRICTIONS
- Violation of Article 8 where contact was restricted to only 4-6 visits/year
- Required specific evidence of harm to justify restrictions
- **APPLICATION:** Contact restrictions must be evidence-based, not blanket policy

### Strand Lobben v. Norway (2019) — LIMITS ON STATE POWER
- Grand Chamber found Norway violated Article 8 by allowing adoption against biological mother's wishes
- Major case limiting state power to sever family ties
- **APPLICATION:** The state cannot override the child-family bond without rigorous justification

### Jirova and Others v. Czech Republic (2023) — FOSTER FAMILY AS FAMILY
- Recognized de facto family life between foster parents and child after 7.5 years
- Long placements CREATE protected family bonds
- **APPLICATION:** Moving a child from a stable foster placement violates Article 8

### Wallova and Walla v. Czech Republic (2006) — POVERTY IS NOT NEGLECT
- Violation of Article 8 when children removed solely due to inadequate housing
- **APPLICATION:** Economic factors cannot justify removal — only actual harm

## 2. INTER-AMERICAN COURT OF HUMAN RIGHTS

### Villagran-Morales et al. v. Guatemala ("Street Children" case, 1999)
- Landmark: States have AFFIRMATIVE obligations to protect vulnerable children
- Right to life includes "access to conditions guaranteeing a dignified life"
- Not merely "don't harm" — the state MUST provide adequate conditions
- **APPLICATION:** States that fail to provide rescue protocol (Paper 110) violate the right to a dignified life

## 3. COUNTRIES THAT BANNED INSTITUTIONAL CARE — AND WHAT HAPPENED

| Country | Action | Result |
|---|---|---|
| **Romania** | Zero children under 2 in orphanages (post-BEIP) | Created national foster care program; outcomes improved |
| **Rwanda** | Law No. 71/2018 — residential care as last resort | Closed genocide-era orphanages, moved children to families |
| **Bulgaria** | Reduced from 7,500 in 168 institutions to ~200 in 4 | **97% reduction** — and outcomes improved |
| **Hungary** | Banned new orphanage placements | Shifted to family-based care |
| **EU broadly** | 2009 policy prioritizing deinstitutionalization | Changed funding rules to discourage institutional investment |

**Universal finding:** Children removed from institutions and placed in family care show "substantial, if incomplete, recovery" across ALL developmental domains. Earlier = better. The BEIP data is unambiguous.

## 4. NATIONAL REDRESS SCHEMES (Governments Admitting Liability)

### Australia — Royal Commission into Institutional Responses to Child Sexual Abuse
- National Redress Scheme: up to **$150,000 per survivor** + psychological treatment costs
- Government acknowledged institutional harm

### Ireland — Industrial Schools and Mother/Baby Homes
- Ex gratia payments of **€84,000 each** to survivors
- State settled with survivors

### Northern Ireland — Historical Institutional Abuse
- Redress Board established for abuse between 1922-1995
- Compensation payments ongoing

**SIGNIFICANCE:** These countries have already admitted that institutional harm to children creates government liability. The legal principle is established. What's new is the QUANTIFICATION — the Wike framework puts a number on the harm.

## 5. THE HELD v. MONTANA FRAMEWORK — "GOVERNMENT IGNORED SETTLED SCIENCE"

### Held v. Montana (2023, affirmed 2024)
- 16 youth plaintiffs (aged 2-18 at filing) WON a constitutional challenge
- Judge Seeley: 103-page decision entirely in favor of youth
- Montana Supreme Court affirmed 6-1 (December 2024)
- **Enshrined "science-based protections for children's fundamental rights"**

**Why this matters for ACE/foster care litigation:**

| Climate Framework | ACE/Foster Care Parallel |
|---|---|
| Government knows fossil fuels cause harm (scientific consensus) | Government knows multiple placements + forced visitation cause harm (ACE data, BEIP, neuroscience) |
| Public Trust Doctrine: duty to protect atmosphere for future generations | Special Relationship Doctrine: duty to protect children in state custody |
| "Deliberate indifference" to known future harm | "Deliberate indifference" to known developmental harm |
| Constitutional right to clean environment | Constitutional right to bodily integrity, substantive due process |
| Children as plaintiffs with standing based on concrete injury | Foster children as plaintiffs with documented placement histories |
| **WON.** | **NOT YET FILED.** |

**Held v. Montana proved that "the government ignored settled science and harmed children" CAN WIN IN COURT.**

The ACE science is MORE settled than climate science. 17,337 participants. 26 years of replication. 50+ countries. R² = 0.987. If Held won on climate, an ACE-based case should win on foster care.

## 6. THE L'AQUILA PRINCIPLE — CRIMINAL LIABILITY FOR IGNORING SCIENCE

### L'Aquila Earthquake Case (Italy, 2012)
- Seven members of Italy's risk commission convicted of manslaughter
- Crime: giving "superficial, approximate and generic" risk analysis before earthquake that killed 309
- Six scientists acquitted on appeal, but ONE GOVERNMENT OFFICIAL'S CONVICTION UPHELD
- **Key reasoning:** The issue was not failing to predict, but providing negligently reassuring risk communication when the science warranted caution

**APPLICATION:** Government officials who reassure the public that foster care placement instability is acceptable — when the science (ACE, BEIP, neuroscience) says it causes measurable, predictable harm — may face criminal liability under this principle. Not for failing to predict harm, but for failing to communicate known risk.

## 7. THE PRECAUTIONARY PRINCIPLE

- Recognized in international and EU law
- "When there are threats of serious damage, scientific uncertainty must be resolved in favor of prevention"
- The BURDEN OF PROOF SHIFTS to the proponent of a harmful activity

**The critical argument:**

If the precautionary principle demands action even under scientific UNCERTAINTY, then failing to act when the science is SETTLED (as with ACEs) represents an even STRONGER basis for liability.

The ACE study findings are not at the frontier of uncertain science — they are at the level of "smoking causes cancer."

Every state that continues practices known from ACE data to cause predictable, measurable harm is making a choice that the science has already judged.

## 8. UN CONVENTION ON THE RIGHTS OF THE CHILD — SPECIFIC VIOLATIONS

### Articles Violated by Current Foster Care Practices

| Article | Right | Violation |
|---|---|---|
| **3** | Best interests paramount | Multiple placements, group homes, forced visitation — none serve best interests |
| **9** | Contact with parents "except if contrary to child's best interests" | Forced visitation with abusers directly violates this exception |
| **12** | Right to be heard | Foster children report being "excluded from decisions about their lives" |
| **19** | Protection from all forms of maltreatment | Placing children with known abusers via forced visitation |
| **20** | Alternative care hierarchy: foster > adoption > institution | Group home placement when foster families available inverts the hierarchy |
| **25** | Periodic review of placement | Multiple placements without proper review |
| **39** | Rehabilitation in an environment fostering health and dignity | Repeated placement disruptions are the opposite |

**Note:** The US is the ONLY UN member state that has NOT ratified the CRC. But the principles inform international litigation and US state constitutions.
